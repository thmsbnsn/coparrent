# CoParrent Deal Structure And Price Guide

_Last updated: 2026-03-22_

Prepared for:
- **BNSN Solutions**
- **Owner:** Thomas Benson
- **Website:** https://www.bnsnsolutions.com

## Purpose

This is the internal guide for how to talk about price, structure a deal, and avoid weak sale terms.

This is not buyer-facing material.

The goal is straightforward:

- sell the asset cleanly
- keep leverage
- avoid turning a product sale into a vague support obligation

## What This Should Be Sold As

The cleanest framing is:

- **asset sale**
- **product acquisition**
- **software/IP transfer with short transition support**

That is the strongest fit for what CoParrent is today.

It should **not** be framed as:

- an acqui-hire
- a consulting engagement
- a promise to help build the buyer's roadmap for months
- a mature SaaS business with proven revenue multiples

## What The Buyer Would Potentially Receive

Depending on the final deal, the package can include:

- source code
- repository history
- product documentation
- buyer package documents
- deployment and environment documentation
- Supabase schema, edge functions, and frontend code
- test suite and verification notes
- design assets that are actually owned and transferable
- domain and brand assets, if included in the transaction
- short transition support after close

Treat domains, brand assets, and post-close support as value-bearing items, not freebies.

## What Should Not Be Included By Default

Do not give these away casually:

- unlimited support
- custom feature development
- major rebrand implementation work
- long-term contractor availability
- revenue or growth guarantees
- broad warranties that go beyond what you can honestly stand behind

If the buyer wants ongoing help, price it separately.

## Pricing Posture

The valuation work in `docs/SELL_READINESS_PLAN.md` still stands:

- likely fair value after the readiness work is complete: **$100,000-$225,000**
- realistic midpoint target: **about $165,000**
- higher outcomes usually require better buyer competition, early revenue, or stronger market proof

That is the valuation context.
Negotiation posture is a different question.

## How To Talk About Price Early

Do not lead with a number in the first outbound message.

If a buyer asks too early, use language like this:

"I expect the right deal to land in the low-to-mid six figures depending on scope, transfer assets, and how much transition support is included."

That keeps the conversation open without anchoring too low.

## Recommended Ask Strategy

### If buyer fit is strong and the package is clean

Reasonable opening zone:

- **$225,000-$275,000**

Why:

- it leaves room to negotiate
- it still sounds serious instead of fantasy pricing
- it matches the amount of product definition and engineering a buyer would avoid rebuilding

### If the buyer is interested but clearly price-sensitive

Likely defendable zone:

- **$150,000-$225,000**

This is the zone most aligned with the current asset profile if the process is clean but buyer pressure is limited.

### Quiet internal floor

Do not advertise this.

But unless there is a very specific reason, drifting below roughly:

- **$100,000-$125,000**

starts to look like selling a lot of finished product work too cheaply.

### Stretch pricing

Pushing above:

- **$275,000**

usually needs one or more of these:

- two serious buyers in motion at once
- clear strategic fit
- signed pilot interest
- early revenue
- a buyer who urgently wants speed

Without that, a very high ask can kill momentum.

## Best Deal Shape

Best-case deal structure:

- majority cash at close
- clear asset list
- short transition period
- narrowly defined founder assistance
- no vague future obligations

If there is a transition period, define it tightly:

- 2-4 weeks
- fixed hours or fixed calls
- clear end date

Anything beyond that should be separately priced.

## Earnout Guidance

Avoid earnouts unless the buyer is otherwise strong and the terms are simple.

If an earnout is unavoidable:

- keep it short
- keep it objective
- keep it tied to measurable events
- avoid buyer-controlled gray areas

Bad earnouts are where good software deals go to die.

## Exclusivity Guidance

Do not give long exclusivity early.

If a buyer wants exclusivity:

- it should follow real diligence progress
- it should be time-limited
- it should come with a serious signal, ideally an LOI or other concrete step

A vague request for exclusivity mostly just kills your leverage.

## NDA Guidance

An NDA is fine once the conversation is real.

Do not let paperwork become a substitute for buyer seriousness.

The normal order should be:

1. short outreach
2. one-pager
3. call
4. NDA if needed
5. deeper demo and diligence material

## Red Flags

Watch for these:

- buyer wants full repo access before a serious conversation
- buyer keeps asking for more material but avoids a call
- buyer wants months of support bundled into the price
- buyer shifts the discussion toward custom development work
- buyer wants equity instead of cash
- buyer wants open-ended indemnity
- buyer wants exclusivity with no real commitment
- buyer keeps pushing for a "quick number" before understanding scope

Those are usually signs of a weak process or a weak buyer.

## What To Keep Separate In Negotiation

Keep these as separate levers:

- codebase and IP
- brand and domain
- post-close support
- buyer onboarding / handoff help
- additional implementation work

If everything gets lumped into one vague number, you lose room to negotiate.

## What To Say If A Buyer Pushes Low

Good response:

"I understand the need to be disciplined on price. My view is that the real value here is the amount of product and vertical work already done. If the fit is real, I am happy to talk about scope and structure, but I am not looking to price this like a loose code dump."

That keeps the tone calm without getting defensive.

## What To Avoid Saying

Do not say:

- "Make me any offer"
- "I just want to move it"
- "I do not care about structure"
- "I can stay on as long as needed"
- "The number is flexible" before they have earned flexibility

All of that weakens the process.

## Suggested Sale Process

1. confirm fit
2. send one-pager
3. run a call or demo
4. answer first-round questions
5. decide whether the buyer is serious enough for deeper diligence
6. share tighter materials
7. negotiate scope and price
8. move to LOI or draft purchase agreement

Do not go deep with everyone.

## Bottom Line

The money will not come from sounding excited.

It will come from:

- having a real asset
- packaging it cleanly
- talking to the right buyers
- keeping the structure disciplined

The right posture is calm, specific, and willing to walk away from weak terms.
